Why Does Turkey Experience So Many Earthquakes?

Tectonic Position

Turkey sits at the meeting point of three major tectonic plates: the Eurasian, African, and Arabian plates. This unique position creates constant geological pressure.

Major Fault Systems

The North Anatolian Fault (NAF) and East Anatolian Fault (EAF) are two of the most active fault lines in the world, running across Turkey for hundreds of kilometers.

Historical Pattern

Turkey experiences approximately 500 earthquakes per year on average, with several being strong enough to cause damage and casualties.

The Science Behind It

The Arabian Plate is pushing northward into the Eurasian Plate at about 2-3 cm per year. Meanwhile, the African Plate moves in a different direction. Turkey, caught in the middle, experiences tremendous stress along its fault lines.

The North Anatolian Fault is a right-lateral strike-slip fault, similar to California's San Andreas Fault. It has produced devastating earthquakes throughout history, including the 1999 İzmit earthquake (M7.6) and the recent 2023 Kahramanmaraş earthquakes.

The East Anatolian Fault extends for about 580 km and was responsible for one of the two major earthquakes in February 2023, highlighting how both fault systems remain highly active.
