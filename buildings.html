The Truth Behind Building Collapse

In Turkey's most devastating earthquakes, the biggest destruction came from preventable design flaws. During the 2023 Turkey-Syria Earthquakes, which were responsible for over 50,000 deaths, the worst damage came from buildings that were already unsafe.

If we want to save lives in future earthquakes, we have to understand why so many buildings failed in the first place. This section breaks down why so many structures failed and the solutions that could've prevented thousands of them from collapsing.

Why So Many Buildings Failed

Turkey's 2023 earthquakes were not just devastating because of the strong shaking; many buildings themselves were already weak. Insufficient construction, materials, and inspections made structures more likely to fall.

The most common issues were:

  • Soft-story buildings, where open spaces for retail or parking caused the upper floors to collapse downward
  • Low quality concrete destroyed by ground movement
  • Incorrect placements and lack of steel reinforcements
  • Safety rules that were ignored, and poor inspections that failed to catch these issues

Constructing Earthquake-Safe Buildings

Earthquake-safe buildings are designed to move without collapsing. Instead of staying stiff, they use structural elements that can bend and absorb shaking. This allows the structure to stay standing even during strong earthquakes.

Key features of earthquake-resistant design include:

  • Ductility: materials and connections that can bend without breaking (stretchable steel reinforcements)
  • Shear walls: stiff walls that prevent swaying
  • Strong columns & reinforced beams: keep floors tied together as one unit.
  • Proper rebar placement: correct use of steel reinforcements that hold concrete together during shaking

Building a Safer Future

Fortunately, it's very possible to reduce the risk of future disasters. Turkey can move forward by strengthening existing buildings and making sure new ones are built safely.

Here's what actually makes a difference:

  • Retrofitting older structures with braces and shear walls.
  • Ensuring that builders use stronger materials instead of cutting corners
  • Following seismic building codes in new builds
  • Conducting real and transparent inspections
  • Making sure the government enforces the rules and ensures unsafe buildings aren't approved
